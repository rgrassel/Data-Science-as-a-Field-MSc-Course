Description of the Data

The data used is titled NYPD Shooting Incident Data (Historic). As per the official government website: “This is a breakdown of every shooting incident that occurred in NYC going back to 2006 through the end of the previous calendar year. This data is manually extracted every quarter and reviewed by the Office of Management Analysis and Planning before being posted on the NYPD website. Each record represents a shooting incident in NYC and includes information about the event, the location and time of occurrence. In addition, information related to suspect and victim demographics is also included.”

Day vs Night Shooting Incident Analysis

From the charts it appears most of the shooting incidents occur during the night time. To test this hypothesis, the day was broken into two 12-hour time periods. Day is designated as the hours between 06:00:00 and 18:00:00, and Night are the remaining 12 hours. The column Night has a 0 for Day and a 1 for Night. Given there are only two categories, a one proportion Z-test will be performed to determine if the number of incidents occuring during the night are significantly different than those occuring during the day, given a theoretical proportion of 50% Day and 50% Night. The null hypothesis is that the proportion of shooting incidents that occured during Night = 0.50 and the alternative hypothesis is the propotion of shooting incidents occuring during the Night > 0.50.

A right-tailed z-test will be performed. H0: p(Night) = 0.50 Ha: p(Night) > 0.50

The test statistic is calculated as:

z = (p-p0) / √p0(1-p0)/n

where:

p: observed sample proportion p0: hypothesized population proportion (0.50) n: sample size

The resulting p-value < 2.2e-16 and test shows that we can reject the null hypothesis and accept the alternative hypothesis that the number of shooting incidents at night is greater than 50% of the total incidents.

# One proportion Z-test
prop.test(x = sum(data_cleaned$NIGHT == 1), n = sum(data_cleaned$NIGHT == 1) + sum(data_cleaned$NIGHT == 0), p = 0.5, correct = FALSE, alternative = "greater")

Seasonality Model

The data appears to have a seasonality component, particularly with the summer months. To test for the effect of individual months on shooting incidents, a dummy variable is created for each month except January. Additionally, a model is made to predict the next month’s number of incidents. The model consists of a lagged variable of the previous year’s average monthly incidents and the monthly dummy variables.

Yo = b0 + b1X1 + b2X2 + b3X3 + … b11X11 + b12X12 + error

Where: Y0 = monthly incidents b0 = intercept b1X1 = previous year’s average monthly incidents b2X2 = February Dummy Variable b3X3 = March Dummy Variable … b12X12 = December Dummy Variable

# Create seasonal2 which uses numeric months
seasonal2 <- data_cleaned %>% group_by(YEAR, MONTH_NUMERIC) %>% summarise(MONTHLY_INCIDENTS = n()) %>% ungroup()
## `summarise()` has grouped output by 'YEAR'. You can override using the
## `.groups` argument.
# Group by Year to get yearly shooting incidents
monthly_average <- data_cleaned %>% group_by(YEAR) %>% summarise(AVERAGE_MONTH_INCIDENTS = n()/12) %>% ungroup()
#lag monthly averages
monthly_average$LAGGED_AVERAGE_MONTH_INCIDENTS <- lag(monthly_average$AVERAGE_MONTH_INCIDENTS)

# Join seasonal and yearly
joined_seasonal = merge(x=seasonal2, y = monthly_average, by = "YEAR", all.x = TRUE)
# Use factor() to create dummy variables for the months
month_model = lm(MONTHLY_INCIDENTS ~ LAGGED_AVERAGE_MONTH_INCIDENTS + factor(MONTH_NUMERIC), data = joined_seasonal)
summary(month_model)

The summary shows that in comparison to the baseline month of January, the months of May, June, July, August, and September are statistically significant at the 0.001 level, with October being statistically significant at the 0.01 level. The summer months of June, July, and August have the greatest coefficients, with July being the highest at 87. This means in the model the month of July has 87 more shooting incidents than January.The LAGGED_AVERAGE_MONTH_INCIDENTS is also statistically significant at the 0.001 level and the coefficient of 0.69741 means that the predicted month is 0.69741 of the average monthly incidents of the prior year. Finally, the model has a decent predictive power with an adjusted R-squared of 0.5737 and a statistically significant F-statistic.

Conclusion

Data was taken from the NYPD Shooting Incident Data (Historic) database. This study chose to focus on the time and seasonal effect of shooting incidents and only the relevant information from the database was used. When displayed based upon time of the day, the number of shooting incidents clearly increases with darkness as most of the incidents occur during the evening/night/early morning. To test this hypothesis a one-proportion z test was used. The shooting incidents were divided into two categories. Any incident that occured during 06:00:00 to 18:00:00 was labeled as “Day” and everything outside those hours was labeled as “Night”. The results from the test showed that we can reject the null hypothesis and accept the alternative hypothesis that the majority of the shooting incidents occur during the Night. A model was made to predict the number of shooting incidents in a month.The number of shooting incidents showed a clear seasonal component when looked at on a monthly basis. This was accounted for in the model by implementing dummy variables. The model used the average monthly number of shooting incidents from the prior year while controlling for monthly seasonality to make its predictions. The results showed in comparison to the baseline month of January, the months of May, June, July, August, September, and October had statistically significantly more shooting incidents. The summer months of June, July, and August had the most incidents. Controlling for this monthly seasonality allowed the model to have decent predictive power, except for the year 2020 in which the decades-long downward trend of shooting incidents ended with a dramatic increase in 2020. In summary, this shows the nighttime hours and summer months have the greatest number of shooting incidents. Utilizing this information may allow the NYPD to better tailor their police response and prevent shooting incidents in the future.

Biases

I did not choose the general topic or original dataset in this study. However, choosing to focus on the time and seasonality aspect shows my biases. I have a natural belief, one that in all likelihood society at large generally shares, that being out when it is dark and being out at late hours of the night is more dangerous than during the middle of the day. When a crime occurs during the day we often hear “can you believe it happened in broad daylight?” or some similar phrase. To mitigate this bias I specifically avoided making the “Night” window too narrow and cherry-picking the latest hours of the night. The “Night” window is the same number of hours as the “Day” window and covers the evening and morning when there is some light depending on the time of year. The other large bias I have is that I live in New York City and we are working on a NYPD dataset. I have noticed the increase in crime over the last several years and how much less safe I feel on a daily basis. From my personal life, I strongly believe NYC has become a more dangerous place. To help avoid this bias, I chose to study seasonality and not specifically if crime had increased in the last several years. To avoid adding a bias on my views of the boroughs in NYC (Manhattan, Brooklyn, etc.) I did not use those in my analysis. I have also heard before that more crime is committed during summer months. It also makes sense on a basic level. Warmer weather makes it easier to be outside, out late a night, to gather with groups, etc. Harsh winters in NYC make many things in life harder, including crimes. To mitigate this bias, I made dummy variables for each month instead of just the summer period. This way, if months in the fall or winter had an affect it would show up in the data as well.
